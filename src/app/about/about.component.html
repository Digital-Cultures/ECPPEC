ABOUT THE ECPPEC PROJECT

Eighteenth-Century Political Participation and Electoral Culture

The ECPPEC project explores how people participated in parliamentary elections in England, in the period from 1695 to the Reform Act of 1832 when modern democracy was being shaped. Few were entitled to vote. But elections often generated an explosion of print, speeches and songs; processions, assemblies and entertainments; and even new modes of dress, decoration and behaviour. ECPPEC explores how women and men, children and adults, poor and rich, franchised and unenfranchised, all participated in elections – as voters, as consumers, but also as the active makers of these extraordinary cultural and political experiences.


ECPPEC collects a wealth of information in one place for the first time, including which elections were ‘contested’ and where poll books survive. For the constituencies selected as case studies, the project presents the ‘big data’ available from poll books to show who voted, what their backgrounds were, who they voted for, and how this varied across time and place.
